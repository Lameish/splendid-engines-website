How I’m Getting 10x More Done With an Xbox Controller, Wireless Headphones, and Claude

And you can too.

My buddy is a car guy. I am not. I told him I was thinking about a cold air intake for my GTI because they sound fun.

“You’ll need a performance exhaust, too,” he said.

“Why?”

“Because you’ll be pulling in more air. You need somewhere for it to go.”

He was almost right.

A cold air intake alone adds horsepower. It works on its own. But once you upgrade the intake, the exhaust becomes the new bottleneck. Speed up one part of a system, and you find the next slow part.

That, it turns out, is also the story of my career. And the reason I’m writing this on a wireless headset, walking laps around my office, with an Xbox controller in my hands.

The intake was never the problem

My brain runs hot and wide. Getting it all out has always been the bottleneck.

For decades, the exhaust was a keyboard. Before that, a pencil. Now it’s a wireless headset, an Xbox controller, and Claude. I’m getting about ten times more done than I did a year ago.

This piece is how, why, and how to do it yourself.

If you’ve ever felt your brain running ahead of your fingers, or watched the AI moment unfold wondering whether it was built for someone other than you, or you can’t sit still: keep reading. Skip ahead to the setup if you want. The philosophy is here for the curious.

One disclaimer

I run Splendid Engines with my business partner Brandon. We’re a small team, intentionally. Brandon and I share Claude conversations across our work. We treat it as the larger team we don’t have to hire.

My brain runs hot and wide. It chases dopamine. It’s curious. It always has been. When something bores me, I drift. That’s a blessing and a curse: I’ve ended up with a broad set of interests and a working understanding of a lot of things. I know enough to be dangerous. I know enough to speak. I’m not an expert in anything.

My degrees: cultural studies (BA, English lit minor), digital media (MA), game design (postgrad cert). I work from home.

Take what follows as one person’s experience, not a controlled study. If your work or your brain is shaped differently, your mileage will vary.

Bottleneck one

The hand

I first got unbottlenecked in tenth grade. My handwriting was a disaster. Teachers told me my ideas were good but my essays were unreadable. The school let me bring a laptop to class. Grades jumped. The ideas hadn’t changed. The exhaust had.

Stanford put numbers on it later. A 2017 study found voice dictation was three times faster than smartphone typing, with twenty percent fewer errors. The desktop case favors voice even more: typing tops out at sixty words a minute for most people, and most people are not most people.

When my words leave my mouth and land on the page without the typing in between, my brain doesn’t have to wait. Intake and exhaust finally match.

Bottleneck two

The chair

The next thing that matched up was the walking.

A 2014 Stanford study had 176 people do creative-thinking tasks while sitting and while walking. The walkers consistently produced more creative output. The effect held indoors and out. Movement was the variable, not the scenery.

There’s a parallel finding in pediatric research on ADHD. A twenty-minute bout of aerobic exercise gives kids measurable boosts in attention, behavior, and academic performance. Adult studies replicate it. Bodies that run the way mine does always knew this. I just used to think it was a bug.

I always hated standing desks. They felt like punishment. I’d rather sit. But standing was trying to solve the wrong half of the problem. Standing while typing is still typing. The exhaust was still the keyboard.

Now I walk the office. Wireless headphones on, Xbox controller in hand, pacing between the kitchen and the desk, talking to Claude. The work runs at the speed my brain wants to run.

Bottleneck three

The expert

I’m a generalist. Cultural studies, digital media, a little game design, a lot of running things. The world used to ask me to go deeper in one of them. AI doesn’t. AI asks me to know where the connections are.

A 2023 NBER study tracked 5,179 customer support workers using a generative-AI tool. Productivity rose 14 percent on average. For the least experienced workers, it rose 34 percent. The AI did the expertise lifting. Less-experienced humans got the biggest amplification.

That number is doing more work than it looks. Generalists function like novices in any given domain: there’s a lot we don’t know in depth. AI fills it in. Ethan Mollick makes a similar case in Co-Intelligence: the humanities-soaked reader who prompts in interesting language becomes the better operator.

People used to ask if generalists had a place in tech. I think the answer changed.

So here’s where I am

I’m not at a desk most of the time. I’m walking. My ideas leave my head through my voice, not my hands. The expert in the corner used to be the only one who could ship. Now I’m shipping, and the AI is the expert. I’m faster, more accurate, less tired, and having more fun.

If your brain has been running ahead of your fingers your whole life, here’s your performance exhaust. The setup is below.

The fewer people you try to impress, the more impressive you become.

The setup

Here’s the whole rig.

Mine is Mac. Windows works too, with a Windows equivalent of Controlly (a quick search will find one).

Hardware

  • An Xbox controller. Any Bluetooth model.
  • Wireless headphones with a decent mic. I use Bose 700s. Any solid pair works.

Software

  • Claude desktop. Built-in shortcuts: ⌘1 for Chat, ⌘2 for Cowork, ⌘3 for Claude Code.
  • Controlly ($3.99 on the Mac App Store, 7-day free trial). Maps the controller to keyboard shortcuts.
